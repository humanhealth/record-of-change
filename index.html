The record of change · a strategy brief

Health AI gives better answers. We still need to know whether anything changed.

Google has the models, the research, the apps and the data. What's missing is smaller and duller: a record of what was tried for one person, and what happened after. That is the layer that turns the capability into health, and shows where it never reaches.

You can always find what was done. What happened after is a mess.

Surfaces where it creates value

Patient

Value before any clinician is involved

  • See what you tried and what changed.
  • One safe next action.
  • Works with wearables, symptom and medication notes, goals and follow-up prompts.
  • A memory of what happened, not just advice.

Clinician

Value even when patient use is partial

  • A short baseline, action, response and barrier summary.
  • Less noise from patient data, because it is structured around an attempt.
  • Follow-up that asks the right question.
  • Supports shared decisions, without claiming to diagnose on its own.

System / population

Value once aggregated

  • Shows where follow-up breaks.
  • Shows which barriers recur.
  • Shows who got advice but not benefit.
  • Supports service redesign, outreach and equity measurement.
  • Turns "advice given" into "benefit reached, or not".

Research

Value over time

  • Observational patterns first.
  • Hypotheses about who responds and who does not.
  • Better outcome definitions.
  • In time, n-of-1, pragmatic or point-of-care randomised designs where appropriate.
  • A practical unit of learning for a learning health system.

What already exists

You can always find what was done. A statin started, a scan ordered, weight loss advised, a referral sent. What happened after is patchy: a stray repeat test, a follow-up note, a questionnaire score if you are lucky. Scattered, often in free text, often stuck to one pathway, and rarely tied back to the thing it was meant to change.

That is not an accident. The record was never built to tell you whether someone got better. It was built for billing, handover, safety and the courts. So it doesn't.

Google has built a lot of the hard machinery near this. Research embedded with clinical and product teams, across imaging, genomics, mobile sensing and public health1. Open medical models in MedGemma, which Google says to validate before trusting in any one setting2. AMIE, a diagnostic chat system now tested in a real clinic with Beth Israel Deaconess3. Medical speech4, reasoning and orchestration in Gemini5, discovery upstream in AlphaFold6. And a way for people to hold their own records, in Health Connect, which now carries basic medical data in FHIR with the person's permission7. The consumer health and Fitbit surfaces sit alongside8.

Plenty of capability. None of it, on its own, tells you whether a particular try changed anything.

What is missing

There is no small, steady unit that says, for one person and one try: here is where they started, here is what we tried, did it actually happen, what changed, what got in the way, what next.

Call it a record of change. For one person it proves nothing. You never see the version of them who skipped the statin. No counterfactual. Fine. The early claim is small: this is what we tried, and this is what followed. It is still more than we keep now.

What the record adds

It ties the try to what came after. One person, one attempt, written down properly: where they started, what was tried, whether they could do it, what moved, what blocked it, what to do next.

Not a new medical record, not a trial, not a stand-in for a clinician. Just the thin bit between the advice and the result.

Why it works without full system adoption

It's modular, and that's the point. One person can keep it and get something out of it before any clinician is involved: a memory of what they tried and what changed, and one safe next step. One clinic can run it without full patient uptake, building it from ordinary notes, tests and, where the person shares it, Health Connect data. One pathway can use it alone.

It's worth more as these join up. It doesn't need them all at once, and it doesn't need a hospital to commit before a patient sees anything.

Why it matters for equity

Put a lot of these together and things show up that are hard to see now. Where follow-up breaks. Which barriers keep coming back. Who got the advice and never got the benefit.

Same advice, different chances. One person can act on it. Another can't: cost, pain, night shifts, no money, fear. Write down only what was said and the two look identical. Write down what changed and you can see who got left behind, while there's still time to do something.

It doesn't fix that. It makes it visible, which is the first thing you need before you can act on it.

How it matures into stronger evidence

The causal stuff comes after. Watch first: patterns, then questions about why people respond differently, then better outcome definitions. Then, where a setting is ready and it's safe, the stronger designs: n-of-1, pragmatic trials, randomising at the point of care.

Those methods were never the hard part. Following people up was. That's what made good evidence slow, dear, and rarest for the people who needed it most. A cheap record of what was tried and what followed is what they were always waiting on.

What it is not

  • Not another model.
  • Not an autonomous doctor.
  • Not proof of cause from one person.
  • Not a replacement for medical records, trials or PROMs.
  • Not something that needs every hospital signed up first.

Theory of change

Write down the tryOne person, one attempt, as a small structured unit.
Structure what followsTurn scattered notes, tests and patient data into a before, a try, a result and a blocker.
Give it backA memory and a next step for the person; a short summary for the clinician.
Add them upSee where follow-up breaks and where the benefit never lands.
Then ask the harder questionsWhere it's safe and earned, move from watching to testing.
Knowing more is not the hard part anymore. Knowing whether it worked is.
